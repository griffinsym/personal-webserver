Project Overview

  • Designed and simulated diode-based rectifier circuits using LTSpice to model DC behavior and AC rectification.
  • Selected and justified a commercial diode from DigiKey based on forward voltage, current rating, and cost; verified datasheet specifications.
  • Performed DC sweep and transient simulations to extract diode I–V characteristics and validate analytical calculations.
  • Designed half-wave and full-wave rectifiers with filter capacitors to achieve ≤200 mV ripple voltage across a 10 kΩ load.
  • Compared analytical and SPICE results, documented peak/valley voltages, ripple voltage, and diode current performance within rated limits.

Parts 1&2 - Diode Selection and DC Analysis

The 1N914 silicon diode from onsemi was chosen for its low cost, availability, and suitable forward voltage range of 0.62–0.72 V. A DC sweep from -12 V to +12 V in LTSpice showed current conduction beginning near 0.64 V, matching the datasheet values. The diode safely operates within the ±12 V range, making it ideal for the rectifier circuits used later in the project.

Part 3 - Half Wave Rectifier

This system has 3 primary operations. They are as follows:
A half-wave rectifier using a 12 V, 60 Hz AC source and 10 kΩ load was analyzed. Theoretical peak output was 11.36 V with a current of 1.136 mA, while simulation results were 11.397 V and 1.139 mA. The 0.3% difference confirms strong agreement between hand calculations and SPICE results.

Part 4 - Full Wave Rectifier

A full-wave bridge rectifier was tested with the same source and load. Calculations predicted a 10.72 V peak and 1.072 mA current, while simulation gave 10.80 V and 1.080 mA. The circuit provided a smoother output than the half-wave design and showed less than 1% error from theory.

Part 5 - Filtered Rectifiers

Filter capacitors were added to both rectifiers to reduce ripple below 200 mV. The half-wave circuit used a 100 µF capacitor with 0.187 V ripple, and the full-wave circuit used 50 µF with 0.197 V ripple. Both circuits stayed within the 1 A surge current limit, with the full-wave design producing a steadier DC output.
